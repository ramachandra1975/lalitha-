Prefatory Note
आरम्भवाक्यम्
Opening Statement

The Lalitopākhyāna ("The Episode of the Blessed Lalitā") is the crowning jewel of the Brahmāṇḍa Purāṇa's Uttarabhāga (final section). Embedded within it is the incomparable Lalitā Sahasranāma — one thousand nāmas of the Great Goddess transmitted by Hayagrīva to the sage Agastya at Kāñcīpuram. The Upākhyāna opens with three dhyāna verses whose every word is a yantra, every syllable a mantra. This Part I presents:

  1. Every significant Sanskrit term from the maṅgalācaraṇa, nāmadheyam, and three dhyāna verses with multi-layer commentary.
  2. All bījākṣaras (seed-syllables) and mātṛkā-sounds embedded therein, with Bhāskararāya's Saubhāgyabhāskara and Śaṅkara's Advaitic readings.
  3. Their neuroacoustic, bioacoustic, and anatomical significance from modern neuroscience, psychoacoustics, and biofield research.
  4. Correspondences with the Saptasvara system of Indian musicology.
"Parāśakti alone is the śabdabrahman — she is simultaneously the utterer, the utterance, and the reality uttered. Every syllable of her nāmas is a vibratory gate to that reality."
— Bhāskararāya Makhin, Saubhāgyabhāskara, Prastāvanā
Section I
नामावली — संस्कृतपदविश्लेषणम्
The Sanskrit Terms: Complete Lexical & Śāstric Analysis

The invocatory section presents four textual layers: (1) the Gaṇeśa-obeisance, (2) the Śrī Mīnākṣī invocation (maṅgalācaraṇa), (3) the heading Lalitā Sahasranāma, and (4) three dhyāna-ślokas. Each Sanskrit term is analyzed below.

1.1 · THE MAṄGALĀCARAṆA INVOCATION

ॐ श्रीललितामहात्रिपुरसुन्दरीस्वरूपा
श्रीमीनाक्षी परमेश्वरी परदेवताम्बिकायै नमः
Oṃ Śrī-Lalitā-Mahā-Tripurasundarī-Svarūpā
Śrī-Mīnākṣī Parameśvarī Paradevat-Ambikāyai Namaḥ
ॐ Oṃ (Praṇava) Mūla Bīja
Padārtha (Word-Meaning)
Derived from the verbal root ava (to protect, to shine) with the augment a — the Praṇava is the primordial sound preceding all articulate speech. It is simultaneously a single syllable (ekākṣara), a trisyllabic compound (A+U+M), and a fourth state of pure resonance (turīya-nāda).
Bhāskararāya (Saubhāgyabhāskara)
Bhāskararāya Makhin (1690–1785 CE), the supreme commentator of the Sahasranāma, identifies Oṃ in the Śrī Vidyā context as the sarva-bīja — the seed of all seeds. He maps A to Brahmā/the gross body (sthūla), U to Viṣṇu/subtle body (sūkṣma), M to Rudra/causal body (kāraṇa), and the anunāsika resonance (anusvāra) to Parāśakti herself as Turīyā. In the Pañcadaśī context, Oṃ prefixes Hrīṃ as Oṃ Hrīṃ Śrīṃ, forming the gateway to Śrī Vidyā sādhanā.
Śaṅkarācārya (Māṇḍūkyabhāṣya via Gauḍapāda)
In the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad Bhāṣya tradition, Śaṅkara (via Gauḍapāda's Kārikā) establishes Oṃ as Ātmabrahman itself. The four pādas of Oṃ correspond to the four states of consciousness: Viśva (waking/A), Taijasa (dream/U), Prājña (deep sleep/M), and Turīya (transcendent/ardhamātrā). Śaṅkara's Advaita collapses the distinction between the meditator and Oṃ — "Ātmaivedam sarvam — ayam-Ātmā brahma."
Tātparya (Inner Significance)
Every recitation of the Sahasranāma begins with Oṃ because it is the sonic form of the Goddess as Vāgdevī. It is the resonance of the Śabdabrahman manifesting as the universe — the Mātṛkā in her undivided state before she differentiates into the 51 akṣaras of the Sanskrit alphabet. The Oṃ before every invocation is an acknowledgment that all language is ultimately Her body.
ललिता Lalitā Central Nāma
Padārtha
From the root laḷ (to play, to sport, to be graceful) + kta suffix → lalita = "she who plays/sports/acts gracefully." Feminine form Lalitā. The root carries connotations of spontaneity, beauty, ease, and playfulness unencumbered by effort (ayāsa). She is the cosmos as divine play (līlā).
Bhāskararāya
In the Saubhāgyabhāskara on nāma #1, Bhāskararāya gives the nirukti: Lalati krīḍati iti Lalitā — "She who sports is Lalitā." He additionally references the Vāmakeśvara Tantra reading: Laṃkāraḥ parabrahmarūpaḥ, Liṃkāraḥ sthitikārakaḥ, Taṃkāraḥ kāmarūpaśca — iti Lalitā nirucyate — a bīja-by-bīja deconstruction of the name where La = Paramātman, Li = Sustainer, Ta = Kāmarūpa (the form of desire). The name thus encodes the Triple Śaktis: Icchā, Jñāna, Kriyā.
Śaṅkarācārya (Saundaryalaharī context)
In the Saundaryalaharī, attributed to Śaṅkara and heavily commented upon by Lakṣmīdhara, the Goddess's graceful sport is identified with the Śivanandalaharī — the wave of Śiva's bliss. Śaṅkara's advaitic reading: Lalitā is the dynamic aspect of the Nirguṇa Brahman; her "play" is the apparent universe which has no independent reality — mithyā — yet appears as real as long as ajñāna persists.
Bhāvārtha (Esoteric Significance)
In the Śrī Vidyā tradition, Lalitā is the personification of the Kāmakalā — the triangular geometry of Desire-Knowledge-Action that underlies all manifestation. Her name is the sonic yantra of the Śrī Cakra's bindu (central point), from which all 43 sub-triangles emanate. To utter "Lalitā" is to invoke the entire structure of the cosmos.
महात्रिपुरसुन्दरी Mahā-Tripurasundarī Mahā Nāma
Padārtha
Mahā = great (superlative of greatness). Tri = three. Pura = city/body/world/state. Sundarī = beautiful woman (from sundar = beautiful). Compound: "The supremely beautiful Goddess of the Three Cities/States." The three Puras are variously: (a) the three bodies (sthūla, sūkṣma, kāraṇa), (b) the three states of consciousness (jāgrat, svapna, suṣupti), (c) the three Guṇas, (d) the three Bindus of the Kāmakalā, (e) the three Vedas.
Bhāskararāya
Bhāskararāya in the Varivasya Rahasya gives the most complete analysis: the three Puras are the three Bindus — the Rakta Bindu (Śakti, triangular, below), the Śveta Bindu (Śiva, circular, above), and the Miśra Bindu (their union, mixed, center). Mahātripurasundarī is the beauty (sundaratva) that is revealed when these three merge in the Parā Bindu at the apex of the Śrī Cakra. Additionally, she is "Sundarī" because her form is constituted of pure Ānanda — bliss is the only aesthetic category that fully captures her nature.
Śaṅkarācārya
In the Advaita reading of the Tripurā Upaniṣad (which Śaṅkara is said to have commented upon), Tripurasundarī is Nirguṇa Brahman itself, described as beautiful only by metaphor since pure consciousness has no form. The three "cities" are Māyā's three powers: Āvaraṇa (veiling), Vikṣepa (projection), and Ānandasvarūpa (blissful ground). The Goddess's beauty is the Ānanda of the self recognizing itself.
श्रीमीनाक्षी Śrī-Mīnākṣī Kṣetranāma
Padārtha
Mīna = fish. Akṣī = eye. "She whose eyes are fish-shaped." In classical Sanskrit poetics (kāvyaśāstra), the fish-shaped eye is the supreme ideal of feminine beauty — eyes that are long, curved, and luminous like the rohita fish. The Mīnākṣī Stotram describes her eyes as holding all worlds within them.
Bhāskararāya (Varivasya Rahasya)
In the Tantric context, Mīna (fish) symbolizes the pair of Śaktis — Icchā and Jñāna — moving freely in the water of consciousness (cidākāśa). The two eyes are the Sun and Moon (sūrya-candra), which in the Kuṇḍalinī framework correspond to Iḍā and Piṅgalā nāḍīs. Mīnākṣī's gaze is the simultaneous activation of both solar and lunar channels, the state of Suṣumnā.
Tātparya
As the presiding deity of Madurai (Mathurā of the South), Mīnākṣī represents the Śrī Vidyā tradition's southernmost seat. The Lalitopākhyāna begins at Kāñcī — where Agastya receives the teaching — and the invocation of Mīnākṣī links Kāñcī to Madurai, the two great Śākta pīṭhas of Tamil Nadu. Agastya himself is the founding sage of both Dravidian grammar and Tamil Śaivism.
परमेश्वरी परदेवताम्बिका Parameśvarī Paradevat-Ambikā Compound Epithets
Padārtha
Parameśvarī: Parama (supreme) + Īśvarī (female lord/controller). "The Supreme Sovereign Goddess." Paradevata: Para (transcendent) + Devatā (divine being). "The transcendent Divinity." Ambikā: from the root amb (to go, to be luminous) + ikā — "The luminous Mother." Also < ambā (mother in common usage).
Bhāskararāya
Bhāskararāya in the Saubhāgyabhāskara distinguishes Parameśvarī from Īśvarī: while Īśvarī implies sovereignty within Māyā (Mahāmāyā), Parameśvarī is sovereignty that transcends Māyā — she is the svātantryaśakti of Parameśvara Śiva, not a power subordinate to him but identical with his essential freedom. Paradevata specifically indicates she cannot be subsumed under any category — not Vaiṣṇavī, not Śāktī, not Śaivī — she is Para (beyond all).
Śaṅkarācārya (Devī Aparādha Kṣamāpaṇa Stotra)
In this stotra attributed to Śaṅkara, the Goddess is addressed as Parāmbike — the supreme mother who cannot be accessed through the normal routes of Vedic ritual or Yoga alone, only through total surrender (śaraṇāgati). Ambikā as "mother" in Śaṅkara's advaitic understanding is not a relational term but an ontological one — she is the matrix (mātṛ) of all experience, the ground in which all arisings occur.

1.2 · THE THREE DHYĀNA VERSES — COMPLETE ANALYSIS

Dhyāna I: Sindūrāruṇa Verse

सिन्दूरारुणविग्रहां त्रिनयनां माणिक्यमौलिस्फुरत्
तारानायकशेखरां स्मितमुखीमापीनवक्षोरुहाम् ।
पाणिभ्यामलिपूर्णरत्नचषकं रक्तोत्पलं विभ्रतीं
सौम्यां रत्नघटस्थरक्तचरणां ध्यायेत्परामम्बिकाम् ॥
सिन्दूरारुण-विग्रहा Sindūrāruṇa-vigrahā Rūpa-Varṇana
Padārtha
Sindūra = vermilion (lead sulfide / mercuric sulfide, HgS — the reddest of red pigments). Aruṇa = the deep red of dawn (from the root ṛ — to move, the redness of moving light). Vigraha = body, form (vi + graha = that which is distinctly grasped). The body of the Goddess is the color of vermilion at dawn — a specific hue deeper than scarlet, warmer than crimson, vibrating between the visible red spectrum and infrared.
Bhāskararāya
In the Saubhāgyabhāskara's commentary on nāma 7 (Raktavarṇā — she who is red-colored), Bhāskararāya provides the Tantric rationale: the color red (rakta) symbolizes Rajas — the creative power, the menstrual blood of the Goddess through which all life flows, the Kuṇḍalinī in her active state. Sindūra specifically is sacred because it contains mercury (Pārada) — the substance identified with Śiva's semen in the Pārada Saṃhitā — bonded with sulfur, identified with Śakti. The Goddess's body is literally the union of Śiva-mercury and Śakti-sulfur: HgS = Śiva-Śakti sambandha rendered material.
Śaṅkarācārya (Saundaryalaharī, verse 43)
Verse 43 of the Saundaryalaharī reads: Nakhair nāka strīṇāṃ karajaiḥ… — Śaṅkara repeatedly describes the Goddess's body as radiating a red glow that transforms the sky itself into sindūra. Lakṣmīdhara's commentary explains that the red body is the Vāmā Śakti aspect — the left, creative, emanating face of the Goddess. This redness is associated with the Agni tattva but also with Vimarśa — the self-reflective awareness of Brahman.
Tātparya
The first word of the dhyāna, Sindūra, is itself a meditational anchor. Red at ~620–750nm wavelength activates the reticular activating system and hypothalamic-pituitary axis. The Goddess's red form is not merely aesthetic — it is a specific instruction for retinal-hypothalamic entrainment during dhyāna: visualize red, and the nervous system enters a state of aroused-yet-inward wakefulness appropriate for Kuṇḍalinī meditation.
त्रिनयना Trinayanā Trikāla Dṛṣṭi
Padārtha
Tri = three. Nayana = eye (from nī, to lead/guide — the eye that guides). "She with three eyes." The third eye is located at the ājñā-cakra, the center of the forehead between the eyebrows, corresponding anatomically to the pineal gland.
Bhāskararāya
On nāma 577 (Trinayanā) in the Sahasranāma, Bhāskararāya gives three meanings: (1) the three eyes are Sūrya (right, day, Piṅgalā), Candra (left, night, Iḍā), and Agni (center, fire, Suṣumnā); (2) the three eyes are the three Bindus — Rakta, Śveta, Miśra — the Kāmakalā geometry; (3) the three eyes are the three Vedas (Ṛk = right, Sāma = left, Yajus = third). The Tantrārāja Tantra adds that the third eye blazes with the fire of Mahā Jñāna that destroys Tripura — the Pura of ignorance, the Pura of ego, the Pura of karma.
Śaṅkarācārya
In Śaṅkara's Śivānandalaharī (and the Saundaryalaharī tradition), the third eye is pure Viveka — discriminative awareness. While the two ordinary eyes see duality (this and that), the third eye sees only Brahman. It is the jñānacakṣus — the eye of wisdom — that opens when the Goddess's grace illumines the practitioner's antaḥkaraṇa.
Neuroscience & Anatomy
The ājñā-cakra's anatomical correlate, the pineal gland, produces melatonin and regulates circadian rhythms. Modern pineal research (Strassman, Callaway) suggests the pineal also secretes trace quantities of dimethyltryptamine (DMT) under specific neurochemical conditions. The third eye in meditation corresponds to activation of the retino-hypothalamic tract and increased gamma-band (40Hz+) activity in the prefrontal cortex — the neural signature of non-ordinary awareness states documented by Lutz, Newberg, and others.
माणिक्यमौलिस्फुरत्-तारानायकशेखरा Māṇikya-mauli-sphurat-Tārānāyaka-śekharā Śiroabhūṣaṇa
Padārtha
Māṇikya = ruby (from Māṇikya = gem that creates wonders/manas-stimulating). Mauli = crown of the head, crest (lit. the topmost, from mū). Sphurat = flashing, gleaming, vibrating. Tārānāyaka = lord of stars = Moon (Candra). Śekharā = she who wears as crest ornament. Full compound: "She who wears on her ruby-crowned head the flashing crescent Moon."
Bhāskararāya
The ruby crown (māṇikya-mauli) corresponds in the Śrī Vidyā system to the Sahasrāra cakra — the crown of a thousand petals. Bhāskararāya in the Saundaryalaharī commentary notes that the ruby's color (deep red, ~694nm) is the color of the Śakti at her highest point of ascent — the Parā Nāda. The crescent moon on the crown is the Amā-kalā — the 16th and invisible phase of the moon that never wanes, symbolizing the nectar of immortality (amṛta) secreted by the Soma in the Sahasrāra, which flows downward to nourish all the lower cakras.
स्मितमुखी Smitamukhī Karuṇāmūrti
Padārtha
Smita = gentle smile (from smi, to smile slightly — a smile that does not fully open, carrying mystery and compassion simultaneously). Mukha = face (lit. the front, from muc, to release — the face releases expression). "She whose face bears a gentle smile."
Bhāskararāya
The smita (gentle smile) as opposed to hāsa (laughter) is significant: Bhāskararāya notes that the Goddess's expression is praśāntakaruṇā — a serene compassion that is neither active intervention nor cold detachment. This is the expression of one who knows that all suffering is ultimately illusory, yet responds to it with infinite tenderness. In the Tantra, this smita is the Śākta analog of the Buddha's archaic smile — it represents the state of sahaja samādhi, natural/spontaneous absorption.
Neurological Note
Research in psychophysiology (Ekman, Davidson) shows that the gentle Duchenne smile — engaging the orbicularis oculi (around the eyes) not just the zygomatic muscles — activates the left anterior prefrontal cortex and releases endorphins. Meditating on the Goddess's smile (smita) while reciting her nāmas activates mirror neurons in the practitioner's premotor cortex, inducing a physiological analog of the smile — literally transferring neurological peace from the dhyāna-mūrti to the meditator's nervous system.
आपीनवक्षोरुहा Āpīna-vakṣoruhā Śakti-Pratīka
Padārtha
Āpīna = full, swollen, round (from ā + pīna = completely filled). Vakṣas = chest, breast (from vakṣ, to increase/nourish). Ruha = that which grows (from ruh, to grow). "She with full, rounded, nourishing breasts." The breasts of the Great Goddess are cosmologically significant — they are the source of the nectar (amṛta) that sustains the universe.
Bhāskararāya
In commenting on nāma 97 (Stanabhāra-dalanmadhya — she whose waist bends under the weight of her breasts), Bhāskararāya connects this to the Kāmakalā: the two breasts are the two Śiva Bindus of the Kāmakalā geometry — the upper two points of the downward triangle — between which the Yoni Bindu (the Śakti point below) completes the triangle. The breasts are thus not merely physical but yantrātmaka — the living geometry of creation. Their fullness signifies the infinite capacity to nourish all beings — all 8.4 million species of life (caurāsī-lakh jīvas) are nursed by the Goddess.
रत्नचषक — रक्तोत्पल Ratna-caṣaka · Raktotpala Hasta-Āyudha
Padārtha
Ratna = gem, jewel (that which pleases, from ram). Caṣaka = cup, vessel (ca + śaka = the enclosing thing). Ali = bee / black bee (symbolizing the soma-drinking yogin). Pūrṇa = full. Rakta = red. Utpala = lotus that grows above water (ut + pala). Right hand: gem-studded cup brimming with nectar (honey/soma) around which bees hover. Left hand: a blood-red lotus.
Bhāskararāya
The gem-cup filled with nectar (ali-pūrṇa ratna-caṣaka) is the Cidagnikuṇḍa — the fire-pit of consciousness from which Lalitā is born according to the Lalitopākhyāna's own mythology. The nectar it holds is the Soma-rasa of the Sahasrāra — the bindu-amṛta that flows when the Kuṇḍalinī reaches the crown. Bees (ali) represent the yogins and adepts who drink this nectar through their meditation — they are madhupa (honey-drinkers), a standard epithet for advanced Śrī Vidyā upāsakas. The red lotus in the other hand is the Mūlādhāra cakra — showing that the Goddess simultaneously holds the origin and the culmination of Kuṇḍalinī.
Śaṅkarācārya (Saundaryalaharī v. 8–9)
Verses 8–9 of the Saundaryalaharī describe the Goddess's hands holding weapons that also grant liberation. The nectar-cup specifically is the Mahāpādukā of grace (anugraha) — she holds destruction potential (with the noose and goad) and simultaneous liberation potential (with the lotus and nectar), showing that she is Saguṇa Brahman who appears as both kāla (death/time) and amṛta (immortality).

Dhyāna II: Aruṇā Verse

अरुणां करुणातरङ्गिताक्षीं
धृतपाशाङ्कुशपुष्पबाणचापाम् ।
अणिमादिभिरावृतां मयुखैः
अहमित्येव विभावये भवानीम् ॥
करुणातरङ्गिताक्षी Karuṇā-taraṅgita-akṣī Dṛṣṭi-Viśeṣa
Padārtha
Karuṇā = compassion (from kṛ = to do + uṇā = that which acts in response to suffering). Taraṅgita = made into waves, rippling (from taraṅga = wave). Akṣī = two eyes. "She whose eyes ripple/overflow with waves of compassion." The compound is one of the most beautiful in Sanskrit literature — the eyes are not merely compassionate, they are taraṅgita: waves arise in them as water in a vessel vibrated by sound.
Bhāskararāya
The karuṇā of the Goddess is not ordinary pity. Bhāskararāya distinguishes it from kṛpā (mercy that requires a petitioner) and dayā (sympathy requiring suffering to be witnessed): karuṇā is the spontaneous, causeless overflow of love that does not await petition. The term taraṅgita is key — the waves in the eyes indicate that this compassion is a natural vibration, a resonance of her nature, not a response to external stimuli. This is the niṣkāraṇa kṛpā — causeless grace — which is a cornerstone of Bhāskararāya's understanding of Śrī Vidyā sādhanā.
पाश-अङ्कुश-पुष्पबाण-चाप Pāśa · Aṅkuśa · Puṣpabāṇa · Cāpa Catur-Āyudha
Padārtha
Pāśa = noose, rope (from paś = to bind, to see). Aṅkuśa = elephant goad (from añc = to bend + kuśa). Puṣpabāṇa = flower arrows (the five arrows of Kāma/Manmatha made of five flowers). Cāpa = bow made of sugarcane (ikṣu-cāpa). These four are the standard weapons of Lalitā in the Pañcadaśī tradition.
Bhāskararāya (Saubhāgyabhāskara, detailed)
In the Saubhāgyabhāskara, Bhāskararāya gives the most extensive symbolic analysis of these four weapons:

Pāśa (Noose): Represents Icchā Śakti — the power of desire/will. It binds (bandha) those who approach the Goddess with impure intention, and liberates (mokṣa) those who surrender. Ultimately the Pāśa is Māyā itself — the binding power of the cosmic illusion. The Goddess holds Māyā (she is not held by it).

Aṅkuśa (Goad): Represents Jñāna Śakti — the power of knowledge that corrects, guides, and steers. As an elephant goad directs the enormous power of an elephant, Jñāna Śakti directs the enormous power of Kuṇḍalinī through the suṣumnā channel without allowing it to dissipate into the external senses.

Puṣpabāṇa (Flower Arrows): The five arrows are the five Tanmātras — Śabda (sound/ether), Sparśa (touch/air), Rūpa (form/fire), Rasa (taste/water), Gandha (smell/earth). They are flowers because sense experience, when directed toward the Goddess, becomes a form of worship rather than entanglement. The Kriyā Śakti, the power of action, is represented here.

Cāpa (Sugarcane Bow): The mind (manas) itself is the bow. Sugarcane (ikṣu) is chosen because the mind is sweet (pleasurable-driven), flexible (bends easily to desires), yet contains the essence of sweetness (rasa) — consciousness — within. When the mind is the bow and the arrows are the sense objects, liberation consists in turning this entire apparatus toward the Goddess herself.
Śaṅkarācārya (Saundaryalaharī vv. 10–11)
Śaṅkara's Saundaryalaharī verses 10–11 describe exactly these weapons in the Goddess's hands. The advaitic reading: the noose, goad, bow, and arrows are not external weapons but internal psychological processes — binding (pāśa = attachment), correction (aṅkuśa = discrimination), desire (cāpa = the mind), and cognition (bāṇa = the cognitive acts). The Goddess wields these not to harm but to demonstrate mastery: she holds the entire psyche in her hands, free.
अणिमादिभिरावृतां मयुखैः Aṇimādibhir āvṛtāṃ mayūkhaiḥ Aṣṭa-Siddhi
Padārtha
Aṇimā = the siddhi of becoming infinitely small (from aṇu = atom). Ādi = beginning (indicating all the eight Siddhis). Āvṛtā = surrounded by, encircled. Mayūkhaiḥ = by rays of light (mayūkha = ray of light, flame, from ma + uṣa). "Surrounded by the rays [in the forms] of Aṇimā and the other [Siddhis]."
Bhāskararāya
The Aṣṭa Siddhis (eight supernatural powers) are: Aṇimā (atomic diminution), Mahimā (infinite expansion), Garimā (infinite heaviness), Laghimā (infinite lightness), Prāpti (reaching anywhere), Prākāmya (irresistible will), Īśitva (lordship/control of elements), Vaśitva (subjugation of all). Key Phrase: aham ity eva
The culminating phrase of this verse — aham ity eva vibhāvaye Bhavānīm — "I meditate on Bhavānī as my very Self (aham)" — is philosophically the most significant of the three dhyānas. This aham-bhāvanā (I-feeling meditation) is the heart of Śrī Vidyā: the practitioner does not visualize the Goddess as other but identifies with her. This is the Tādātmya-dhyāna — identity-meditation — which Bhāskararāya says is the highest form of Śaktopāya. Śaṅkara's Advaita similarly culminates in aham Brahmasmi — I am Brahman — here rendered as aham Bhavānī.

Bhairavī — the rāga of deep emotional surrender, often sung at dawn. The komal (minor) svaras create a tender vulnerability. Pre-dawn to Sunrise; Late monsoon
Dhyāna II
Aruṇā Karuṇā		 Karuṇā (compassion) + Vīra (heroic). The weapons of control + the compassionate eyes + the identity-meditation "aham ity eva" — this is simultaneously fierce and tender. Kalyāṇī — all śuddha (natural) svaras except teevra MA (augmented fourth), creating a sense of complete beauty and resolution. The rāga of auspicious energy. Yaman (= Kalyāṇī equivalent) — associated with evening calm, the divine descending grace, the opening of awareness. Evening (Sandhyā); Early autumn
Dhyāna III
Padmāsana Hemābhā		 Śānta (peace) + Bhakti (devotion). The golden goddess, the peaceful form, the giver of all wealth — this is the rasa of fulfilled devotion, all desires extinguished in loving surrender. Todi — the rāga of the solitary seeker, deep introversion, and longing for the divine. Its komal (flat) RI, GA, DHA create a yearning quality that resolves into peace. Bhairavi (morning) / Bageshri — the rāga of completed longing, sweetness, and devotional love. Sung late at night — the completion of the spiritual day. Deep night (Niśītha); Spring

4.3 · SAPTASVARA AND THE SEVEN CAKRAS — SONIC YOGIC ANATOMY

The Nāda Yoga tradition (as articulated in the Nāda Bindu Upaniṣad, the Haṭhayoga Pradīpikā's 4th chapter on Samādhi via Nāda, and the Śāradātilakatantra's phonemic body mapping) establishes a complete mapping between the seven musical notes, the seven cakras, and the seven primary bījakṣaras:

Saptasvara — Saptacakra — Saptabīja: The Threefold Sonic Body

The fundamental insight of Nāda Yoga is that the human body is a vīṇā — a stringed instrument — with the Suṣumnā nāḍī as its primary string, the seven cakras as its seven frets, and the Kuṇḍalinī as the plectrum. When the Sahasranāma is chanted with full dhyāna (visualizing the Goddess's form as described), the voice produces the saptasvara sequence that activates each cakra in ascending order. The dhyāna verses themselves are structured to guide this ascending activation:

  • SA (Ṣaḍja / La): The opening "La" of Lalitā establishes the ground note — Mūlādhāra — Earth stability.
  • RI (Ṛṣabha / Va): "Vakṣas" (breasts), "Ratna-caṣaka" (nectar cup) activate the fluid/water cakra — Svādhiṣṭhāna — creative energy.
  • GA (Gāndhāra / Ra): "Aruṇā," "Rakta," "Ra-"-sounds throughout — fire of Maṇipūra — will and transformation.
  • MA (Madhyama / Pa): "Pāśa," "Padma," "Parā" — the heart-lotus of Anāhata — love and compassion (karuṇā-taraṅgitā).
  • PA (Pañcama / Ha): "Hemābhā," "Hayagrīva," "Ha-"-sounds — the golden voice of Viśuddha — pure expression of the divine name.
  • DHA (Dhaivata / Kṣa): "Tri-nayana," "Tārānāyaka" — the third eye of Ājñā — transcendent vision.
  • NI (Niṣāda / Oṃ): "Māṇikya-mauli," "Padmāsanasthā," the final Praṇava — crown of Sahasrāra — dissolution into the infinite.

4.4 · NĀDA BRAHMAN — SOUND AS THE ULTIMATE REALITY

The philosophical synthesis of this entire analysis converges on a single point that both Bhāskararāya and Śaṅkara — despite their apparent differences on the question of the Goddess's ultimate status — agree upon:

"Śabda brahma param brahma — the Śabda Brahman is the Para Brahman. The universe did not emerge from the Brahman and then become sonic; it has always been sound all the way down. The Sahasranāma is not a description of the Goddess — it is the Goddess. Each nāma is her body; each syllable is her living pulse."
— Bhāskararāya Makhin, Saubhāgyabhāskara, opening invocation commentary

Śaṅkara, in the Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (verse 61), states: Śabdo'pi brahmaiva — "Sound too is Brahman alone." The entire creation is an ābhāsa (apparent appearance) within the pure luminosity of Brahman. The Sahasranāma as sound is thus not a path toward Brahman but Brahman's own self-utterance within the apparent world of time and space. To hear it, chant it, or meditate on it is to participate in Brahman's own self-recognition.

From the neuroscientific perspective, this is corroborated by the finding that the human auditory cortex is the most "predictive" of all sensory cortices — it constantly generates internal models of expected sounds and updates them with incoming auditory data. Chanting the Sahasranāma reconfigures these predictive models at the deepest levels of auditory cortex organization, gradually rebuilding the practitioner's entire sonic map of reality around the Goddess's names and qualities. Over time, every sound heard in the external world begins to resonate with the internalized vibratory templates of the mātṛkās — the practitioner begins to hear the Goddess in all sound. This is the neuroscientific correlate of the Tantric state called Śabda-brahma-anubhava — direct experience of Brahman-as-Sound.
